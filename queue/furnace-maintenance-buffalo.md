## Fix #1: Clean the Flame Sensor (Eliminates Most "Won't Ignite" Calls)

**What it is:** The flame sensor is a thin metal rod — usually about 2 inches long, sometimes with a white porcelain base — mounted near the burner in your furnace. Its job is to confirm that the burner has actually lit when the furnace fires. Over time, oxidation builds up on the rod's surface. When this oxidation layer gets thick enough, the sensor can no longer "see" the flame, and the furnace shuts itself off as a safety measure.

The result: your furnace tries to start, the burner lights briefly, and then the whole system shuts down. It may try two or three times and then go into lockout mode. You call for service.

**The fix:**

1. Turn off power to the furnace at the breaker or the furnace's own power switch.
2. Turn off the gas supply to the furnace.
3. Let the furnace cool for 15 minutes.
4. Open the furnace access panel (usually just lifts off or has two screws).
5. Locate the flame sensor — follow the wires from the control board; the sensor is typically a small metal rod near the burner assembly, held by a single screw.
6. Remove the one screw and gently pull the sensor out.
7. Using a piece of fine steel wool or a lightly abrasive pad (not sandpaper — too aggressive), gently rub the metal rod portion until it looks clean and shiny. This takes 30 seconds.
8. Reattach the sensor, close the panel, restore gas and power.
9. Set your thermostat to call for heat. The furnace should light and stay on.

**Safety note:** If you smell gas at any point, stop immediately, leave the house, and call National Fuel Gas at 1-800-365-3234 or 911. Never work on gas equipment while a gas odor is present.

## Fix #2: Use the Right Filter (MERV 8–11 Is Your Target)

Walk into any Home Depot or Lowe's in the Buffalo area and you'll find furnace filters labeled with MERV ratings from 1 to 16. MERV stands for Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value — it measures how fine a particle the filter captures. Higher is better for air quality, right?

Not for your furnace.

Most residential gas furnaces are designed for moderate air restriction. Filters with very high MERV ratings — MERV 13 and above — are so dense that they restrict airflow significantly. When airflow drops, your heat exchanger can overheat, your system runs longer to achieve the same temperature, and you can actually shorten the life of your blower motor.

**The optimal range for most Buffalo residential furnaces is MERV 8–11.** This provides meaningful filtration for dust, pollen, and pet dander without the airflow restriction of higher-rated filters.

Also critical: change your filter on schedule. A dirty filter of any MERV rating becomes more restrictive over time. For most WNY homes, every 60–90 days is the right interval during heating season. Hold your current filter up to a light — if you can't see through it easily, change it now.

Pleated filters in the MERV 8–11 range are available at Home Depot (Delaware and Elmwood locations), Lowe's, and hardware stores throughout Erie County for $8–$18.

## Fix #3: Bleed Your Hot Water Radiators

If you have a hot water heating system — the kind with cast iron radiators in each room — and one or more rooms feel cold even when the heat is running, air trapped in the radiator is likely the cause.

Hot water radiators circulate heated water from a boiler. Over time, air accumulates in the system and gets trapped at the top of individual radiators. Air is less efficient than water at transferring heat, so the affected radiator runs cool even with hot water circulating below.

**The fix — bleeding the radiator:**

1. Turn your heat on so the system is circulating.
2. Wait until the radiators are warm (about 15–20 minutes).
3. Locate the bleed valve — a small square or slotted valve at the top, usually on one end of the radiator.
4. Place a small container or towel below the valve to catch water.
5. Using a radiator bleed key (available at any hardware store for $2–$3) or a flathead screwdriver, open the valve slowly counterclockwise.
6. You'll hear a hiss of air escaping. Hold the valve open until water starts to dribble out steadily — this means the air pocket is gone.
7. Close the valve snugly. Don't overtighten.

After bleeding all affected radiators, check your boiler's pressure gauge. If bleeding released significant water along with the air, you may need to add a small amount of water to the system via the boiler's fill valve. Consult your boiler manual or confirm this step with a plumber.

## Fix #4: Clear the Condensate Drain Line

High-efficiency gas furnaces (90%+ AFUE rating — if your furnace has a white PVC exhaust pipe rather than a metal chimney flue, you have one) produce water as a byproduct of combustion. This water drains through a condensate line — a small plastic tube — to a drain or a condensate pump.

When this line clogs with algae, sediment, or debris, the furnace's safety sensor detects the backup and shuts the system down. This is one of the most common autumn furnace failures in WNY.

**The fix:**

1. Locate the condensate drain line — follow the white PVC tube from the furnace.
2. Locate the small condensate trap (a U-shaped loop in the line, usually clear plastic) and the drain end.
3. Disconnect the line at the furnace end (it typically just pulls off a fitting).
4. Use a wet/dry shop vac to suck from the drain end — this clears most simple clogs.
5. Reconnect the line and restore power to the furnace.
6. Pour a diluted bleach solution (1 tablespoon bleach per cup of water) into the line seasonally to prevent algae growth.

## When to Actually Call a Pro

These are the situations where you stop and call a licensed HVAC technician — no exceptions:

- **You smell gas.** Leave the house immediately. Call National Fuel Gas at 1-800-365-3234 (24-hour emergency line) or 911.
- **Your carbon monoxide detector goes off.** Leave and call 911. A cracked heat exchanger can leak CO — this is a life-threatening emergency.
- **You see a cracked heat exchanger.** Look inside your furnace with a flashlight — if the metal heat exchanger has visible cracks, the furnace must be shut down and replaced or repaired by a professional.
- **The furnace error code indicates a pressure switch, draft inducer, or control board issue.** These require professional diagnosis.
- **Any gas line work.** Touching actual gas line connections or valves beyond shutoffs requires a licensed technician.

**National Fuel Gas free furnace inspection:** National Fuel offers a free furnace inspection through their Home Energy Efficiency program for qualifying customers. Call 1-800-365-3234 to ask about scheduling. This is a legitimate service, not a sales pitch — a technician will check safety, efficiency, and flag any issues.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How often should I change my furnace filter in a WNY winter?**

Every 60–90 days during active heating season is the general guideline for a home with standard dust levels. If you have multiple pets, do woodworking or crafts, or have a family member with allergies, 30–45 days is more appropriate. Check your filter monthly by holding it up to a light — when it's visibly gray and you can barely see through it, change it regardless of schedule.

**Q: I tried cleaning my flame sensor and the furnace still won't stay on. What's next?**

If cleaning the flame sensor doesn't resolve the issue, the next most common causes are: a faulty ignitor (the component that creates the spark or glow to light the burner), a failed gas valve, or a pressure switch issue. These require diagnosis with a multimeter and usually replacement parts. At this point, a professional service call is appropriate. Describe what you've already done (cleaned the flame sensor) when you call — a good technician will appreciate that you've eliminated the simplest cause and will move to the next diagnostic step.

**Q: My furnace is making a loud banging noise when it starts up. What is that?**

A banging or booming sound when the burner ignites is called "delayed ignition." It happens when gas accumulates in the combustion chamber before igniting — the larger gas cloud then ignites with a bang. This is often caused by a dirty burner or a weak ignitor. It can also indicate issues with the gas valve. Delayed ignition puts stress on the heat exchanger and should be addressed by a technician. Don't ignore this noise — it gets worse over time.

**Q: How do I know if my old furnace is worth repairing or if I should replace it?**

The standard industry guideline is the "5,000 rule" — multiply the age of the furnace by the estimated repair cost. If the result exceeds $5,000, replacement is usually the better financial decision. For example: a 20-year-old furnace facing a $300 repair = 20 × $300 = $6,000 — replacement is likely smarter. A furnace over 20 years old also has significantly lower efficiency than modern units, which in WNY's long heating season translates to meaningfully higher annual fuel costs.

**Q: Where can I buy a furnace filter in Buffalo without going to a big box store?**

Most hardware stores in WNY carry MERV 8–11 filters. Ace Hardware has locations in Kenmore and Orchard Park. True Value has locations across Erie County. The Unilock/Hardware Plus stores in the region also carry filters. Standard sizes are always in stock; less common sizes may need to be ordered. Know your filter size (length × width × depth, printed on the filter frame) before you go.
