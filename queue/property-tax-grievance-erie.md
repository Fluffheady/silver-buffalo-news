# Erie County Property Tax Grievance: How Buffalo Homeowners Are Getting Hundreds Back (And the Deadline Is Coming)

## Key Takeaways

- Property tax grievances in Erie County allow homeowners to formally challenge their assessment if their home is valued higher than comparable neighborhood sales support.
- The annual Grievance Day for most Erie County municipalities falls on the **4th Tuesday of May** — missing it means waiting a full year to try again.
- The key document is NY State Form RP-524, which you complete and submit to your local assessor's office before or on Grievance Day.
- You can file on your own for free — or hire a tax certiorari attorney on contingency, meaning no upfront cost and no fee if they don't win.
- Average successful grievance in Erie County reduces a homeowner's annual tax bill by **$400–$900**.
- Start by checking your property's assessed value at the NY State ORPS database online, then compare it to recent sales of similar homes in your neighborhood.

Every spring, thousands of Erie County homeowners pay hundreds of dollars more in property taxes than they should. Not because of any wrongdoing — simply because their homes were assessed at a higher value than the evidence supports. And most of them never say a word about it.

Filing a property tax grievance is one of the most underused, legally available financial tools available to Western New York homeowners. The process is designed to be accessible to ordinary people. You don't need a lawyer. You don't need an appraiser. You need a form, some comparable sales data, and about two hours.

Here's exactly how to do it.

## What an Over-Assessment Is (and Why It Happens)

Your local assessor's office determines what your property is "worth" for tax purposes. That assessment is supposed to reflect fair market value — what your home would sell for in an arm's-length sale. Your property tax bill is calculated by multiplying that assessed value by the local tax rate.

The problem is that mass assessments are imprecise. Assessors evaluate thousands of properties at once, often using formulas that can't account for every detail. A home on Hertel Ave that was assessed at $280,000 might actually be worth $240,000 based on what similar homes nearby have been selling for. The homeowner pays taxes on the higher number — unless they challenge it.

Over-assessments happen for several reasons:

- Improvements in the neighborhood raised comparable values, but your property didn't benefit equally.
- Your home has conditions (needed repairs, outdated systems, an unusual layout) that reduce its market value below the neighborhood average.
- The assessor's records contain errors — wrong square footage, wrong number of bathrooms, wrong lot size.
- Your municipality hasn't conducted a full reassessment in years, and outdated values are applied unevenly.

## Step 1: Find Your Current Assessment

Start at the New York State Office of Real Property Tax Services (ORPS) database. You can find it at orps.tax.ny.gov, or search "NY ORPS property lookup."

Enter your address to find your property's:
- **Full market value** (what the assessor thinks it's worth)
- **Assessed value** (often a percentage of market value, depending on the municipality)
- **Exemptions** already applied (such as STAR)

Write these numbers down. You'll need them for your grievance form.

Also check your assessment notice, which most municipalities mail in early spring. This notice triggers your right to grieve — you have until Grievance Day to file.

## Step 2: Find Comparable Sales

Your argument is simple: the assessor thinks my home is worth $X, but similar homes in my neighborhood have been selling for less than that.

You need "comparables" — recent sales of similar homes. Look for properties within a quarter-mile of yours that are similar in size (within 20%), age, bedroom/bathroom count, and condition, and that sold within the past 12 months.

You can find this information for free at:

- **NY ORPS database** (same site as above — sales are public record)
- **Zillow** and **Realtor.com** (recent sale history is visible for most properties)
- **Erie County Clerk's Office** at 92 Franklin St. in downtown Buffalo — sale records are public

Find three to five comparables where the sale price suggests your home's value should be lower than your assessment. Screenshot or print the listings. These are your evidence.

## Step 3: Fill Out Form RP-524

Form RP-524, "Complaint on Real Property Assessment," is available at your local assessor's office or from the NY State Department of Taxation and Finance website (tax.ny.gov).

The form asks for your property details, your estimated fair market value, and the basis for your complaint. Attach your comparable sales evidence. The form is two pages and designed for non-lawyers to complete.

For Buffalo properties, contact the **Buffalo Assessment Office** at **716-851-5733** with questions. For Cheektowaga, Amherst, Tonawanda, and other Erie County municipalities, contact your local town assessor directly.

## Step 4: File on or Before Grievance Day

**Grievance Day for most Erie County municipalities is the 4th Tuesday of May.** However, this varies by municipality — confirm your specific deadline with your local assessor's office well in advance. Missing this deadline means waiting a full year.

You can often file in person or submit your RP-524 by mail. Some municipalities also accept fax or email — confirm the accepted method with your assessor's office.

After Grievance Day, a Board of Assessment Review meets and issues a determination, usually within a few weeks. If they rule in your favor, your assessment is reduced and your tax bill decreases going forward. If they deny your grievance, you still have the option to pursue the matter in Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) court — a low-cost, informal process where many homeowners succeed without an attorney.

## Should You Do It Yourself or Hire an Attorney?

You can file entirely on your own for free. The process is designed for that.

Alternatively, many Erie County homeowners hire a **tax certiorari attorney** who works entirely on contingency — meaning:

- You pay nothing upfront.
- If the attorney wins a reduction, they take a percentage of the tax savings (typically 33–50% of first year's savings).
- If they don't win, you owe them nothing.

For homeowners who find the process intimidating or don't have time to research comparables, this is a low-risk option. The attorney does all the work, and you benefit regardless of their fee.

**Realistic savings:** In Erie County, a successful grievance typically reduces a home's assessed value by $20,000–$40,000. Depending on your local tax rate, this translates to **$400–$900 per year** in reduced taxes — and the lower assessment carries forward into future years unless your municipality reassesses.

## One More Step: Check Your Exemptions

While you're reviewing your assessment, check whether you're receiving all exemptions you're entitled to. Two are especially important for Erie County seniors:

- **STAR exemption (Basic or Enhanced):** The School Tax Relief program provides a reduction in school taxes. Enhanced STAR, for homeowners 65 and older with income under $98,700 (2025 limit — verify annually), provides a larger benefit. If you're not receiving Enhanced STAR and you're 65+, contact the Buffalo Assessment Office at **716-851-5733**.
- **Senior Citizens Exemption (for local property taxes):** Separate from STAR, this provides additional reduction for qualifying seniors based on income. Deadlines and income limits vary by municipality.

Both exemptions reduce your tax bill automatically, year after year, once you're enrolled. Missing them is like leaving money on the table.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I file a grievance even if my home was recently reassessed?**

Yes. A recent reassessment doesn't eliminate your right to grieve. If the new assessment still overvalues your property relative to comparable sales, you can challenge it using the same RP-524 process. The grievance must be filed in the grievance period following the assessment notice — typically the window leading up to Grievance Day in May.

**Q: Will filing a grievance trigger an inspection of my property?**

Generally, no. Filing a grievance does not automatically result in a physical inspection of your home. However, an assessor may choose to verify information, and if your grievance succeeds in reducing your value, it could theoretically flag your property for future review. In practice, most Erie County homeowners who file grievances do not experience any adverse follow-up.

**Q: What evidence do I need if I think the assessor has wrong information about my house?**

If your grievance is based on incorrect data — wrong square footage, wrong number of rooms, a basement the assessor counted as finished when it isn't — bring documentation. A copy of your original building plans, photographs, or a permit history from the city can support your case. Contact the Buffalo Assessment Office at 716-851-5733 to request the assessor's property record card and verify what information they have on file for your property.

**Q: I already filed a grievance last year and it was denied. Can I file again?**

Yes. You can file a new grievance each year during the grievance period. If last year's denial was based on lack of comparable sales evidence, try again this year with stronger documentation. You can also pursue Small Claims Assessment Review (SCAR) within 30 days of a Board of Assessment Review denial — SCAR is an informal court proceeding where many homeowners prevail without an attorney.

**Q: My home is worth more than I thought — can filing a grievance backfire and raise my taxes?**

In New York State, the Board of Assessment Review cannot raise your assessment through the grievance process. You can only receive a reduction or have the grievance denied. The grievance process has a floor but no ceiling — it cannot result in a higher assessment than you currently have.

**Q: I'm a renter, not a homeowner. Can I benefit from this process?**

The grievance process is available only to property owners. However, if your landlord's property taxes decrease as a result of a successful grievance, that can theoretically create downward pressure on rents — though this is not guaranteed. If you believe your landlord's reduced tax burden should affect your rent, that's a conversation to have with them directly.
