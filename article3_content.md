# Buffalo Antique Dealers Are Paying Big for These 9 Items — Check Your Attic Before You Toss Them

**Published by Silver Buffalo News | Nostalgia & Collectibles**

---

> **Key Takeaways**
> - Nine categories of items from the 1960s–1980s are currently fetching serious money from Buffalo-area dealers and online collectors.
> - Many of these items are still sitting in WNY attics, basements, and garages, mistaken for worthless junk.
> - Condition matters enormously — a piece in excellent condition can be worth ten times more than the same piece in poor shape.

Here is something that happens in Western New York every single spring. An estate sale. A basement cleanout. A garage being emptied after decades. And someone carries a box of "old stuff" to the curb — stuff that a dealer two towns over would have paid real money for.

It happens because people do not know what they have. The things that looked ordinary in 1975 look very different to collectors in 2024. And Buffalo, with its rich industrial and sports history, has produced some items that are now genuinely hard to find anywhere else in the country.

Before your next cleanout, read this. One of these nine categories might be sitting in your attic right now.

---

## Item #1: Pre-1990 Buffalo Bills and Buffalo Sabres Memorabilia

This is the category most likely to produce a genuine surprise for WNY households.

The Bills teams of the late 1960s through the 1980s — before the Super Bowl era — are now deeply collectible. Items from the O.J. Simpson era (1969–1977), the Joe Ferguson years, and the early Jim Kelly period (his rookie card is from 1986) are in active demand. A Jim Kelly rookie card in near-mint condition can sell for **$1,000–$3,000** depending on grade. Even lower-grade copies sell for hundreds. OJ Simpson-era Bills items — programs, press photos, signed items — regularly appear at auction.

For the Sabres, the original French Connection line of Gilbert Perreault, Rick Martin, and René Robert (active 1972–1979) drives collector interest. A 1970 inaugural season Buffalo Sabres pennant in good condition sells in the **$65–$150** range. Signed items from Perreault, the team's all-time leading scorer, command more. The original 1970 Buffalo Sabres team pennant is rare because so few survive in decent condition after 50 years.

**What to look for:** Game programs, original pennants, player-issued photographs, autographed items, bobbleheads, and ticket stubs from notable games. The 1973 Stanley Cup Finals appearance and the 1980 Bills AFC Championship season are particularly sought-after.

**Price range:** $25–$3,000+, heavily dependent on player, era, and condition.

## Item #2: Anchor Hocking Fire-King Dishes

This one surprises people who remember these dishes as everyday kitchen items. Anchor Hocking's Fire-King line — produced from the 1940s through the 1970s — has become one of the most actively collected categories in American kitchenware.

The most valuable is **jadeite**: the opaque pale green glass used in mugs, plates, casserole dishes, and baking pans. Fire-King jadeite pieces marked with older block-letter stamps (pre-1960s) are especially valuable. A complete set of jadeite restaurant-style mugs can sell for **$200–$500**. Rare pieces like a Philbe pie plate or a restaurant-style gravy boat can exceed **$1,000**. Museum-quality pieces in original packaging have sold for $5,000 or more, though these are extremely rare.

More common 1960s–70s patterns like Peach Lustre, Wheat, and Primrose are more modest — typically **$5–$50 per piece** — but a complete set of dishes in excellent condition can bring $200–$400 as a lot.

Many Western New York households still have Fire-King in their cabinets or boxes in the basement, because these dishes were advertised as nearly indestructible and they proved it. Check the bottom of any vintage kitchenware for the Anchor Hocking or Fire-King mark.

**Price range:** $5–$1,000+ per piece, depending on pattern, color, and condition.

## Item #3: Vintage Kodak Cameras — Made Right Next Door in Rochester

Rochester, New York — just 75 miles from Buffalo on the I-90 — was the home of Eastman Kodak for well over a century. Kodak was founded there in 1880 and employed tens of thousands of people throughout the region, including many Western New Yorkers who commuted or had family in Rochester.

This gives vintage Kodak cameras a special local significance for WNY households — and a real collector market to match.

The most valuable Kodak cameras from the 1960s–1980s include the **Kodak Retina IIIC** (sells for $150–$250 in working condition), the **Kodak Medalist** (a sought-after medium-format camera worth $200–$600), and various **Kodak Instamatic** models in original boxes ($30–$80). A Cine-Kodak 16mm movie camera in working condition can fetch **$400–$900**. Brownie cameras from the 1950s–60s, though not high-dollar, are easy sells at $25–$80 each and dealers take them readily.

The key is working condition. A Kodak camera in working order with original leather case and instruction manual is worth two to three times as much as the same model in non-working condition.

**What to look for:** Any Kodak camera from the 1950s–1970s. Check closets, camera bags, and boxes of "old electronics." The model name is usually printed on the front of the camera.

**Price range:** $25–$900 depending on model and condition.

## Item #4: Buffalo Savings Bank Coin Banks

Buffalo Savings Bank was a fixture of Western New York financial life for over 150 years before being absorbed by Goldome Bank in the 1980s. The bank gave out promotional coin banks — small ceramic or metal replicas of the bank building or other shapes — to customers who opened accounts, particularly for children's savings programs.

These promotional banks are now collectible locally, driven by WNY nostalgia. Ceramic or metal Buffalo Savings Bank coin banks regularly appear on Etsy and eBay, with prices ranging from **$15–$80** depending on condition and style. The oval advertising banks branded with the Buffalo Savings/Goldome transition are among the most commonly found and sell in the $12–$30 range. Figural banks — shaped like buildings or specific animals — command more.

**Why they matter locally:** Items branded with defunct WNY institutions carry a local nostalgia premium. Buffalo-area collectors and the WNY diaspora (former Buffalonians who moved away) actively seek these items as connections to their past.

**What to look for:** Any small coin bank with "Buffalo Savings Bank" or "Goldome" branding. Check old dresser drawers, toy boxes, and storage shelves.

**Price range:** $12–$80 per bank.

## Item #5: Curtiss Candy Company Memorabilia

The Curtiss Candy Company was founded in Chicago but had major production and distribution ties to the Buffalo area throughout the mid-20th century. Curtiss produced **Baby Ruth** and **Butterfinger** candy bars — two of the most iconic American confections. Pre-1980s Curtiss branded items (tins, wrappers, promotional signs, store displays) are now collected as part of the broader advertising collectibles market.

A vintage Curtiss Baby Ruth tin in good condition can sell for **$40–$120**. Promotional display items and point-of-sale signs are rarer and can fetch $100–$300 at the right auction. Even vintage candy wrappers in excellent condition are collected — sealed or near-sealed examples from the 1960s–70s can sell for $30–$75 each.

**Price range:** $20–$300 depending on item type and condition.

## Item #6: Vintage Tops Friendly Markets Items

Tops Friendly Markets has been a Western New York grocery institution since 1962, when the first store opened in Niagara Falls. Items branded with the original Tops logo — promotional items, employee pins, store merchandise, vintage advertising — have developed a collector following driven entirely by WNY nostalgia.

Tops-branded items are not high-dollar — most pieces sell for **$10–$50** — but they sell reliably to local collectors and the WNY Facebook collector community. Vintage Tops grocery bags, promotional items from store openings, employee anniversary pins, and old grocery receipt books with the original logo are all worth setting aside rather than recycling.

**Price range:** $5–$50 per item. Value is almost entirely local/regional.

## Item #7: Western New York Transit Tokens

Buffalo had an extensive streetcar and bus system throughout the first half of the 20th century, operated through various transit authorities. Transit tokens — the small metal coins issued for fare payment — from the **Niagara Frontier Transit Authority** and its predecessors are collected by transportation history enthusiasts.

A single WNY transit token typically sells for **$5–$25** depending on age, issuing authority, and condition. A collection of 20–30 tokens from different eras can sell as a lot for $100–$200 to the right buyer. Tokens with clear, unfaded lettering and minimal wear command the highest prices. The oldest tokens, from the Buffalo streetcar era of the early 20th century, can reach $30–$60 each.

**What to look for:** Small metal coins, roughly the size of a dime or nickel, often with transit authority markings and "Good For One Fare" text.

**Price range:** $5–$60 per token; $100–$200 for curated lots.

## Item #8: 1970s–1980s Bar Signs from Buffalo Taverns

Buffalo has one of the richest bar cultures in New York State. Neighborhood taverns on every block were staples of working-class Buffalo from the 1940s through the 1990s. When those bars closed or were renovated, their vintage neon and tin advertising signs were often stored, sold, or thrown away.

Signs advertising **Genesee Beer**, **Iroquois Beer** (a Buffalo brand discontinued in 1971), **Simon Pure Beer**, or **Schaefer** with Buffalo bar branding are among the most sought-after. A vintage Iroquois Beer sign in working or restorable condition can fetch **$150–$400**. Genesee-branded signs from the 1970s sell for $75–$200. Generic neon bar signs from the same era sell for $50–$150.

Even non-working neon signs have value — collectors restore them. Old tin bar signs in good condition (minimal rust, legible graphics) also sell well at $30–$100 each.

**What to look for:** Any vintage sign advertising beer, particularly regional WNY brands. Check garages, utility rooms, and barns. Old tin or metal signs with tavern names printed alongside regional beer brands are especially valuable.

**Price range:** $30–$400+ depending on brand, condition, and whether it still works.

## Item #9: 1960s–1970s General WNY Industrial Advertising Items

Buffalo was an industrial powerhouse from the late 19th century through the 1970s. Bethlehem Steel, Republic Steel, Hooker Chemical, and dozens of other major manufacturers had Buffalo-area operations. Corporate promotional items, employee awards, factory-made goods, and advertising pieces from this era are collected as part of the broader American industrial nostalgia market.

Items include: steel company paperweights, Bethlehem Steel promotional calendars, Hooker Chemical desk items, Republic Steel employee anniversary pins, and advertising items from Buffalo-area companies that no longer exist. These typically sell for **$20–$150** each, with the most valuable being items that clearly identify specific WNY companies.

**Price range:** $20–$150 per item.

## Where to Sell in Buffalo

Once you know what you have, here is where to take it:

### Antique World & Flea Market — Clarence, NY
The largest antique marketplace in Western New York, located at **11111 Main Street, Clarence, NY 14031** (716-759-8483). Antique World houses hundreds of dealers in a single location and holds its famous flea market every Sunday from 8am–4pm. It is an excellent place to browse comparable items for price research, or to speak directly with dealers who may make you an offer. Open most days; hours vary by season. Check [antiqueworldmarket.com](https://antiqueworldmarket.com) for current hours.

### Facebook Marketplace and WNY Collector Groups
Facebook Marketplace reaches local buyers directly — no shipping required — and several active Western New York collector groups deal specifically in local memorabilia, vintage kitchenware, sports collectibles, and beer signs. Search for "WNY Collectibles," "Buffalo Vintage," and "Western New York Antiques" in Facebook Groups. Local buyers often pay more than national eBay averages for WNY-specific items because of the nostalgia premium.

### eBay
For items with national collector interest — Kodak cameras, Fire-King jadeite, and pre-1990 Bills/Sabres sports cards — eBay opens your item to a national audience and typically produces the highest prices. Check "sold listings" on eBay (filter to "sold" in the left sidebar) to see what comparable items actually sold for — not just what sellers are asking.

### Maine Antique Digest
[Maine Antique Digest](https://maineantiquedigest.com) is the industry's most-read trade publication for antique dealers and serious collectors. Their online classifieds and auction results section is an excellent research tool for understanding what items are fetching at auction nationwide. If you believe you have a genuinely high-value piece — a working vintage camera, a rare sports item, or a complete Fire-King set — the auction results here will tell you whether a dealer offer is fair.

## A Few Rules Before You Sell

**Never clean a metal or tin item with harsh chemicals.** What looks like "dirt" to you may be original patina that collectors specifically want. Cleaning can destroy value instantly. Wipe gently with a dry or barely damp cloth.

**Keep original packaging.** A Kodak camera in its original box with the manual is worth two to three times more than the camera alone. Do not throw away the box.

**Research before you accept a dealer's first offer.** Dealers buy low and sell high — that is their business model. Check eBay sold listings first so you know the ballpark before any negotiation.

**Photograph everything before moving it.** Good photos of every angle, including marks, stamps, and signatures, give you documentation and make online listings far easier to create.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my old sports item is authentic or a reproduction?

For autographed items, look for certificates of authenticity from recognized services like PSA (Professional Sports Authenticator) or JSA (James Spence Authentication). For unsigned items like pennants and programs, age shows in the paper quality, printing style, and wear patterns — reproductions made in recent decades usually look too crisp and uniform. When in doubt, take the item to a licensed sports memorabilia dealer for a verbal appraisal before committing to a sale.

### Should I clean my Fire-King dishes before trying to sell them?

Wash them gently with mild dish soap and warm water to remove surface dirt — this is appropriate and expected. Do not use abrasive scrubbers, steel wool, or harsh cleaners, which can damage the glaze and reduce value. For jadeite pieces, even small chips or cracks significantly reduce value, so handle them carefully during any cleaning.

### Are there appraisers in the Buffalo area who can value these items?

Yes. Antique World in Clarence is a good starting point — many individual dealers there will give informal opinions on value. For formal appraisals (needed for insurance or estate purposes), look for certified appraisers through the American Society of Appraisers (ASA) or the International Society of Appraisers (ISA). Always verify a formal appraiser's credentials before paying an appraisal fee.

### My old Kodak camera doesn't work. Is it still worth selling?

Yes, often significantly. Many vintage camera collectors specifically restore non-working cameras as a hobby. A non-working camera in otherwise good cosmetic condition — intact leatherette, clear lens, no rust — typically sells for 30–50% of the working price. Include in your listing exactly what you know about why it does not work (jammed shutter, broken film door, etc.) to attract the right buyers.

### How does pricing differ between selling locally in Buffalo versus selling online?

For WNY-specific items (Bills/Sabres memorabilia, local bank banks, regional beer signs), local buyers often pay a nostalgia premium that online national buyers will not. For items with national collector appeal (Fire-King jadeite, Kodak cameras, pre-1990 sports cards), eBay's national marketplace typically produces higher prices than local sales. Use both: sell WNY-specific items locally through Facebook Marketplace or Antique World, and sell nationally-collectible items on eBay.
