# Your Doctor Won't Tell You This: 7 Inflammation "Switches" That Are Aging You Faster in Western New York

**Published by Silver Buffalo News | Health & Longevity**

---

> **Key Takeaways**
> - Buffalo-area residents face a unique combination of environmental factors that can speed up biological aging.
> - Chronic low-grade inflammation — not just disease — is the main engine of early aging.
> - At least 7 specific "switches" common to WNY life can turn inflammation up or down.
> - Most of these switches can be flipped through simple lifestyle changes — no prescriptions needed.
> - Nearly half of Buffalonians are Vitamin D deficient during winter months, according to University at Buffalo research.
> - You can start reversing several of these factors this week, at home, for free or nearly free.

If you have lived in Western New York for most of your life, you already know the winters are brutal. What you may not know is that the very things that define life here — the lake-effect snow, the famous chicken wings, the long gray months indoors — may be quietly aging your body faster than the calendar says.

Scientists call it **biological age**, and it is different from your birth year. Your biological age is how old your cells, organs, and immune system actually act. Two people born in the same year can have biological ages ten years apart — and the difference often comes down to inflammation.

Inflammation is your body's alarm system. In the short term, it fights infection and heals injuries. But when it stays switched on at a low, steady level — what doctors call "inflammaging" — it silently damages blood vessels, brain tissue, joints, and nearly every other system in your body. The good news: inflammation has triggers, and triggers can be managed.

Here are seven inflammation "switches" that are especially active for people living in the Buffalo area — and what you can do about each one.

---

## Switch #1: The Vitamin D Gap That Starts in October

Every fall, something happens in Buffalo that does not happen in Charlotte or Phoenix. The sun drops so low in the sky that its ultraviolet rays can no longer trigger Vitamin D production in your skin. This lasts roughly from October through April — a full seven months.

The result is striking. According to nutrition researcher Peter Horvath at the University at Buffalo, nearly **50 percent of Buffalonians have insufficient Vitamin D levels during winter months**, and 25 percent qualify as clinically deficient.

Why does this matter for aging? Vitamin D is not just a bone mineral. It acts as a hormone that helps regulate your immune system. When levels are low, inflammatory pathways can run unchecked. Low Vitamin D has been linked in research to higher rates of heart disease, cognitive decline, and immune dysfunction — all things that speed biological aging.

**What you can do:** Talk to your doctor about getting a simple blood test (the 25-hydroxyvitamin D test) in October before your levels drop. Most adults need 1,000–2,000 IU of Vitamin D3 daily through supplements during WNY winters. Foods like fatty fish, egg yolks, and fortified milk help, but supplements are usually needed at our latitude. Get outside even on cold days — a 20-minute walk at midday exposes your face and hands to whatever sun is available.

## Switch #2: The "Buffalo Wing Diet" and Your Inflammation Score

Buffalo-style chicken wings are one of the great food inventions of the 20th century. They were born right here, at the Anchor Bar on Main Street, in 1964. But if wings — along with pizza, beef on weck, and fried everything — form the backbone of your weekly diet, your body is paying a price.

The standard Buffalo diet tends to be heavy in **refined vegetable oils, white flour, and processed meats** — all of which are known to raise levels of inflammatory markers in the blood, particularly C-reactive protein (CRP) and interleukin-6.

This is not about shame. It is about biology. These foods tip your body's chemistry toward an inflammatory state, and that state chips away at your biological age day by day.

**What you can do:** You do not have to give up wings. But consider the 80/20 rule — eat anti-inflammatory foods 80 percent of the time. Add more fatty fish (Lake Erie walleye and perch, caught locally, are excellent choices), leafy greens, olive oil, and berries. Turmeric, ginger, and green tea are also well-documented inflammation reducers that are easy to add to any kitchen routine.

## Switch #3: Cold-Weather Inflammation — The Stress Your Body Carries All Winter

When temperatures drop to single digits and you step outside on Delaware Avenue in January, your body goes into a kind of low-level emergency response. Blood vessels constrict. Cortisol rises. Your immune system shifts into a defensive posture.

This is completely normal for a short burst. But in Buffalo, this response can last for months — especially for older adults whose bodies are slower to recover from temperature stress. Chronic cold exposure without adequate warmth management keeps inflammatory cytokines elevated for the entire season.

Slipping on ice makes this worse. Even the *fear* of slipping — the constant muscle tension and cortisol spike that comes from navigating icy sidewalks — is enough to keep your stress hormones elevated. High cortisol over long periods suppresses the immune system while simultaneously driving inflammation in connective tissue.

**What you can do:** Invest in good insulated footwear with traction soles. Yaktrax-style slip-on ice cleats (available at most hardware stores in WNY) dramatically reduce the fear and physical stress of winter walking. Keep your home consistently warm; heating bills are real, but the HEAP program (see our sister article) can help. Warm showers after being outside and warm herbal teas help your vasculature recover faster.

## Switch #4: Seasonal Depression and the Inflammation Loop

Western New York is one of the cloudiest places in the contiguous United States. Buffalo averages fewer than 50 sunny days per year — less than Seattle. The combination of short days and relentless gray skies sets the stage for **Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)**, which affects an estimated 10–20 percent of people in northern climates.

What most people do not realize is that depression and inflammation are deeply linked — they fuel each other. People who are depressed show elevated levels of inflammatory proteins. And people with high inflammation are significantly more likely to develop depression. In WNY, the dark winters can lock you into this loop for months at a time.

**What you can do:** A **10,000-lux light therapy box** used for 20–30 minutes each morning can be as effective as antidepressants for seasonal depression, according to multiple clinical studies. You can find them online for $30–$80. Consistent social engagement — even a weekly coffee with a friend — is a documented inflammation reducer. Exercise, even a brisk indoor walk, breaks the cycle. Many YMCA and community center locations in the Buffalo area offer senior-discounted memberships.

## Switch #5: Blue Light Overexposure During Long Indoor Winters

Because Buffalo winters push everyone indoors for months, WNY residents tend to spend far more time in front of screens than the national average. Televisions, tablets, and smartphones emit **blue-spectrum light** that suppresses melatonin production — the hormone your body uses to regulate deep sleep.

Here is the aging connection: deep sleep is when your brain runs its cellular cleanup cycle. Without it, proteins that drive Alzheimer's-type inflammation accumulate. Poor sleep also raises cortisol, destabilizes blood sugar, and cranks up systemic inflammatory markers across the board. This is a big deal.

**What you can do:** Turn off screens one hour before bed. If that feels impossible, use the "night shift" or blue-light filter setting on your devices. Blue-light-blocking glasses (widely available, under $20) worn in the evening can help significantly. Make your bedroom as dark as possible — even small LED standby lights on televisions or chargers can disrupt melatonin production.

## Switch #6: Gut Health and the Western New York Water Factor

Buffalo's water supply comes from Lake Erie and is treated at the city's filtration plant. The 2023 Buffalo Water Quality Report shows the water is safe by EPA standards — but it is notably hard water, with mineral content that can affect digestion for sensitive individuals. More importantly, the standard American diet common in WNY — low in fiber, high in processed foods — depletes the gut microbiome over time.

Your gut bacteria directly regulate your immune system. When the microbiome is out of balance (a state called dysbiosis), inflammatory signals leak from your gut into your bloodstream in a process researchers call "leaky gut." The result: whole-body inflammation that accelerates biological aging.

**What you can do:** Eat more fermented foods — yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi. Local stores like Wegmans (a WNY institution) carry all of these. Add prebiotic fiber through oats, bananas, onions, and garlic. Reduce antibiotic use to only what is medically necessary, since antibiotics wipe out beneficial gut bacteria. Probiotic supplements can help, but food-based sources are generally more effective.

## Switch #7: Social Isolation — The Silent Inflammation Driver

Research published in the last decade has produced a surprising finding: **loneliness is as damaging to your health as smoking 15 cigarettes a day.** And the inflammation connection is direct. Isolated individuals show measurably higher blood levels of inflammatory proteins compared to people with active social lives.

Buffalo has a strong community culture, but retirement, the loss of a spouse, harsh winters that keep people indoors, and the gradual departure of adult children can all shrink a person's social world. Once that world shrinks, the inflammatory cascade quietly begins.

**What you can do:** Prioritize social connection as a health activity — not a luxury. The Erie County Department for the Aging (716-858-8526) connects seniors to a wide range of local programs, volunteer opportunities, and senior centers throughout Buffalo and Erie County. Faith communities, bowling leagues, card clubs, and neighborhood associations all provide the regular human contact your immune system needs. Even 10 minutes of meaningful conversation per day makes a measurable difference.

## Putting It All Together

You do not have to flip all seven switches at once. Pick the two or three that feel most relevant to your life right now and start there. Vitamin D supplementation in October, a light therapy box, and a 20-minute daily walk would already address four of the seven factors listed here.

Biological age can be reversed. Studies on people in their 60s and 70s show measurable improvements in cellular aging markers within weeks of consistent lifestyle changes. Western New York winters are tough — but the people who live here have always been tougher.

Always verify specific supplement dosages and health decisions with your personal physician or a registered dietitian, especially if you take prescription medications.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is biological age and how is it different from my real age?

Your chronological age is how many years you have been alive. Your biological age is how old your cells, organs, and immune system function. Biological age is influenced by lifestyle, diet, stress, sleep, and environment. Someone who is 65 years old chronologically can have the biological profile of a 55-year-old — or a 75-year-old — depending on these factors. Tests like epigenetic "clocks" can estimate biological age from a blood sample, though these are not yet standard in routine medical care.

### How do I know if I am Vitamin D deficient?

The only way to know for sure is a blood test called the 25-hydroxyvitamin D test. Ask your doctor to include it at your next annual physical, especially if you have it done in late winter. A level below 20 ng/mL is considered deficient; below 30 ng/mL is insufficient. Most researchers studying northern-latitude populations suggest that levels between 40–60 ng/mL are optimal for immune and inflammatory health.

### Is the "Buffalo Wing diet" really that different from other American diets?

In research terms, the standard Western diet — high in refined carbohydrates, processed meats, and seed oils — is already inflammatory. The Buffalo culinary tradition amplifies certain elements of this: deep-fried foods, wings with butter-based sauces, and beef-heavy dishes are particularly dense in pro-inflammatory fats. This does not mean you should never eat these foods. It means that if they form the bulk of your diet week after week, your inflammatory markers will reflect it. Moderation and balance are the practical answer.

### Can I really reduce inflammation without medication?

Yes. The research literature consistently shows that lifestyle interventions — diet changes, regular moderate exercise, improved sleep, stress reduction, and social engagement — produce meaningful reductions in measurable inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha) within weeks to months. Prescription anti-inflammatory medications work faster in acute situations, but for chronic, low-grade inflammation, lifestyle approaches are often more effective and sustainable over the long term.

### What is the best first step for a Buffalo-area senior to start today?

If you can do only one thing, check your Vitamin D status. Ask your doctor for a 25-hydroxyvitamin D test at your next visit, and ask whether a D3 supplement is appropriate for you through the winter months. This single step addresses one of the most pervasive and correctable biological aging factors specific to WNY. If you want to add a second step, get outside for a 20-minute walk every day, regardless of the weather. Dress in layers, wear traction devices on icy days, and walk with a neighbor or friend when possible.
