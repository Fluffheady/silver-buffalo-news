5 Things We Miss About Memorial Auditorium — And One Thing We Don't

By Vinnie DiPaolo
Nostalgia & Home Editor
AI-Assisted Content
April 19, 2026  ·  5 min read  ·  📍 Buffalo, NY Last updated by the Editorial Board: April 19, 2026

📋 Quick Facts

  • Memorial Auditorium — "The Aud" — opened in 1940 and was demolished in 2009 after standing empty for over a decade following the opening of HSBC Arena (now KeyBank Center) in 1996.
  • The building seated around 13,000 for hockey — intimate by modern standards, but widely considered one of the best hockey atmospheres in the league in its heyday.
  • The Aud was also home to the Buffalo Braves NBA franchise from 1970 to 1978 before the team relocated to San Diego.
  • Organist Bob Dill became one of the most beloved figures in Sabres history — fans knew every song he played and responded in kind.
  • Demolition of the Aud was completed in 2009. The site at Main and Terrace was later incorporated into the Cobblestone District area redevelopment.

I have a piece of pale blue plastic sitting on a shelf in my garage. Roughly six inches long, two inches wide, slightly curved. Looks like nothing. A chunk of debris, really. But I know exactly what it is, and I'm not throwing it away.

It's a section of seat plastic from Memorial Auditorium. I pulled it off a broken armrest sometime in the early 2000s, when the city was kicking around conversion plans for the building and my crew had done some preliminary assessment work inside. We were supposed to be looking at structural issues — the roof, the load-bearing situation, whether renovation was viable. I was not supposed to be pocketing souvenirs. But everybody on that crew took something home. Nobody felt bad about it.

The Aud was demolished in 2009. I drove past the site the week after they finished. I'm not going to say I cried, but I'm not going to say I didn't.

Here's what we lost.

1. The Smell of the Concourse

This is the one that hits people first when you ask them. The Aud had a smell that was entirely its own — popcorn, Zamboni exhaust, hot dogs, decades of cigarette smoke baked into the concrete, and underneath all of it something cold and clean that was just ice. You could smell the ice before you could see it.

The concourse was tight and low-ceilinged, which meant the noise and the smells didn't dissipate the way they do in a modern arena with its atriums and food courts and fifty-foot ceilings. Everything hit you all at once when you came through the gate. Before you'd found your seat, before you'd even gotten your program, you knew where you were. There was no other place in Buffalo that smelled like that. There's no place anywhere that smells like that now.

2. Seats So Close You Could Hear the Skates

The Aud held around 13,000 for hockey. KeyBank Center holds over 19,000. You'd think bigger would mean better. It doesn't. Not for hockey.

In the Aud, even from the upper bowl, you were close to the ice in a way that modern arenas simply can't replicate. The sightlines were steep and direct. You could hear the skates cut. You could hear the players talking during stoppages if the crowd went quiet for a moment — which it rarely did. On a big night at the Aud, the sound bounced off that old concrete and came right back at you. It felt like the building itself was alive.

I sat in seats on the glass once — a client gave me his tickets for a Thursday night game in the early 1980s. The puck would hit the boards four feet from my head and the sound was almost painful. It was the best hockey I've ever watched in my life, and it wasn't even a playoff game. Just a regular-season Thursday night in February. That's what the building did.

3. The Buffalo Braves, Before They Were Gone

Younger Buffalonians don't always know this, but the Aud was also an NBA arena. The Buffalo Braves played in that building from 1970 to 1978, and for a brief shining moment in the mid-1970s they were genuinely good — Bob McAdoo won back-to-back NBA scoring titles while wearing a Braves uniform, and the team made the playoffs three straight years.

That building held two professional sports franchises at the same time, sharing the same ice, the same organ, the same concession stands. The Braves relocated to San Diego in 1978 and eventually became the Los Angeles Clippers. Buffalo has not had an NBA team since. The Aud held both of those things — Sabres hockey and Braves basketball — and the loss of both at different times in that same building gives the place a kind of doubled sadness in retrospect.

4. Walking to the Game When Downtown Still Had Life

The Aud sat at Main and Terrace, right at the edge of downtown. And in the 1970s and into the early 1980s, walking to the game meant walking through a downtown that was still a functioning place. The 716 area code wasn't just a phone prefix — it was a world. Main Place Mall was open. The restaurants near Lafayette Square were doing real business. Delaware Avenue still had its grand old buildings occupied and lit up.

You'd leave work or leave the house and you'd walk, because there was something to walk through. The city had density and energy around the Aud that made going to a game feel like an event, not just a sports outing. You were part of something urban and specific. By the time the Aud closed in 1996, that version of downtown had largely faded. The walk to KeyBank Center through the parking lots and empty blocks never felt the same, because it wasn't.

5. Bob Dill and the Crowd That Knew Every Song

Bob Dill was the Aud's organist for decades, and if you were a Sabres fan during his tenure, you knew his music the way you know a song from your wedding. He had a gift for reading the crowd and the moment — a slow build for a power play, a triumphant riff after a goal, the right amount of menace for an opposing penalty. The Aud's organ didn't just fill silence. It conducted the crowd.

And the crowd knew it. When Dill started a familiar riff, the regulars responded before the notes were even finished. It was call-and-response. The building was an instrument and Dill was playing it, and 13,000 people were in on the performance. You can't manufacture that. It takes years of the same people showing up to the same place with the same organist until the whole thing locks into place. That kind of shared knowledge is gone from sports arenas now, and I don't know how you get it back.

The One Thing We Don't Miss: The Men's Bathrooms

I want to be honest here, because this is a publication that prides itself on the truth.

The men's bathrooms at Memorial Auditorium were an experience that no amount of nostalgia can rehabilitate. Trough-style fixtures, perpetually wet floors, lighting that suggested the building's electrical system had given up on that particular room sometime around 1965. The smell during intermission was — well. Let's just say the Zamboni exhaust in the concourse suddenly seemed very appealing by comparison.

Every man who ever attended a Sabres game at the Aud knows exactly what I'm talking about. You timed your trips. You made strategic decisions based on game flow. You held it if the score was close. And when KeyBank Center opened with its clean, tiled, actually-functional restrooms, a small but real part of the collective Sabres fan experience improved measurably.

The Aud deserves to be remembered with love. The bathrooms deserved what happened to them.

If you've still got Aud memorabilia — programs, pennants, old ticket stubs from the Braves or Sabres years — they may be worth more than you think. Vinnie wrote about sports collectibles here, and the principles apply directly to arena memorabilia from torn-down buildings. The scarcity factor is real, and it only grows over time.

Seat plaques, signage, and any authenticated pieces of the building itself (like actual seat hardware) command more. Some recordings of his game performances have surfaced on YouTube and in WNY nostalgia communities on Facebook, though high-quality recordings are rare. If you have recordings of Aud games from the 1970s or 1980s — whether on VHS tape, audio cassette, or any other format — the audio of the organ is in there. WNY nostalgia groups on Facebook are the best place to find what's been preserved.
