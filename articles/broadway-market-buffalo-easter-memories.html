The Broadway Market at Easter: What It Was, What It Still Is, and Why You Should Go This Year

By Vinnie DiPaolo
Nostalgia & Home Editor
AI-Assisted Content
April 19, 2026  ·  5 min read  ·  📍 Buffalo, NY Last updated by the Editorial Board: April 19, 2026

📋 Quick Facts

  • The Broadway Market in Buffalo's Broadway-Fillmore neighborhood opened in 1888, making it one of the oldest continuously operating public markets in the United States.
  • The Easter season — roughly the two weeks before Easter Sunday — is the market's biggest event of the year, drawing tens of thousands of visitors from across WNY and the diaspora.
  • The market's Easter specialty vendors include kielbasa makers, horseradish grinders, hand-painted pysanky egg artists, babka bakers, and sellers of lamb cake molds and forms.
  • Easter 2026 fell on April 5th. If you missed it this year, Easter 2027 falls on March 28th — mark your calendar now.
  • The Broadway Market is located at 999 Broadway in Buffalo's East Side. It is open year-round, not just at Easter, though the holiday season is its cultural peak.

For generations of WNY families with Polish and Ukrainian roots, a grandmother's annual Easter trip to the Broadway Market was a rite of passage — a ritual as fixed as church on Sunday and the blessing of the basket. Visitors came from across Erie County, often by bus, to work their way through every booth. The kielbasa, the horseradish, the hand-painted eggs: each purchase was its own small act of cultural memory. That tradition is still alive at 999 Broadway, and it still draws thousands of families every Holy Week. [2]

What the Broadway Market Is

The building at 999 Broadway was started by a group of citizens on a city-donated parcel and opened in 1888 — that's not a typo, not a rounding. [1] 1888. The market is older than cars, older than the radio, older than the Skyway and Main Place Mall and most of the institutions that Buffalonians think of as permanent.

It sits in the Broadway-Fillmore neighborhood on the East Side, which was the heart of Buffalo's Polish community for most of the 20th century. At its peak, the neighborhood around the market was a dense, working-class Polish Catholic world — the kind of place where the church, the market, and the social club were the three pillars of daily life, and where knowing which parish you attended told people everything they needed to know about you. [1]

The Broadway Market was that neighborhood's pantry. Not just at Easter — year-round. Meat markets, produce, baked goods, dry goods, specialty items you couldn't find anywhere else in the city. But Easter was always its Super Bowl, to borrow a term.

What Easter at the Market Actually Looks Like

If you've never been to the Broadway Market during Holy Week, description only gets you so far — but it's worth trying.

The kielbasa vendors are the first thing that hits you — the smell of smoked sausage and garlic is aggressive and wonderful, and it starts before you're even inside the building. The best vendors grind their own and smoke it on-site, and the difference between that and what you find at a chain supermarket is not subtle. Thousands of customers packed the market for Good Friday 2026 alone, many of them specifically for the kielbasa. [2]

The horseradish grinders are their own category. Visitors consistently describe the fresh-ground horseradish as one of the most sensory experiences at the market — the fresh-ground variety is potent enough to clear sinuses from several feet away, and no jarred product comes close to matching it. It is a staple of the Polish Easter table, and market vendors grinding it fresh maintain a standard that has barely changed in a century. [1]

Then there are the eggs. The pysanky — hand-painted Ukrainian Easter eggs — are something that has to be seen to be understood. Some of the designs are geometric, traditional, hundreds of years old. Some are more recent, more personal. The artists working these booths use beeswax and dye with techniques passed down through multiple generations. You can buy one for a few dollars from a table that looks like it's been set up the same way since 1955, because in most essential respects, it has been.

And then there's the lamb cake. Every Polish Easter table needs a lamb cake — a sweet cake baked in a ceramic or cast-iron mold in the shape of a lamb, frosted white, usually decorated with a ribbon and sometimes a small flag. The molds themselves are sold at the market, alongside the finished cakes. Cast-iron lamb cake molds from the 1940s and 1950s regularly appear at estate sales and antique markets in Cheektowaga and surrounding Erie County towns, often selling for $40–$100 — a testament to how widely these traditions once spread across WNY households.

The Part That Deserves Saying

Easter 2026 fell on April 5th. If you missed it, don't miss 2027: Easter falls on March 28th. [4] Put it on the calendar now.

The women who run many of these booths — the kielbasa vendors, the pysanky painters, the vendors selling kapusta and ćwikła and babka — represent a generation of Polish and Ukrainian grandmothers who learned these trades in an unbroken chain stretching back to the old country. Some are in their eighties. Some have daughters who will continue. Some don't. That specific knowledge — the actual recipe that came over from Kraków or Lviv, the actual hands that have been painting eggs for sixty years — is finite in a way the building is not.

And the building itself is not standing still. The Broadway Market recently received $45 million in state and local renovation funding — one of the largest investments in its 130-plus-year history. [3] The Easter season continues to draw big crowds, with genuine efforts underway to bring new vendors and new energy year-round. The market in 2026 is not a museum, not a dying institution — it's a living one, with real momentum behind it.

For generations of WNY families, the Broadway Market trip was how cultural memory got transmitted — not through textbooks but through tastes, smells, and the patient explanations of the person behind the table. That transmission is still happening. [2] Go next year. Bring somebody younger than you. Explain what you're tasting and where it comes from. That's the whole point.

Frequently Asked Questions

The Broadway Market at 999 Broadway in Buffalo is open year-round, though hours vary by vendor and season. The Easter season — typically the two weeks before Easter Sunday — is when the market is at its fullest and most festive. The Thursday and Friday of Holy Week are peak days; if you want to see the market at its busiest and most traditional, those are your best bets. Easter 2027 falls on March 28th. The market typically ramps up Easter activity in the second week of March.

The essentials for the traditional Polish Easter basket (święconka) that gets blessed at church: kielbasa, smoked ham, a boiled egg, a small piece of bread, salt, and horseradish. The market provides all of these, often at quality you simply cannot find elsewhere. Beyond the basket items: babka (a traditional Easter bread), pysanky (hand-painted eggs) as gifts or decorations, and if you're ambitious, a lamb cake mold and the ingredients to make your own. Budget a few hours, wear comfortable shoes, and arrive hungry.

The Broadway Market opened in 1888, making it one of the oldest continually operating public markets in the United States — older than most American institutions people think of as historic. Its longevity reflects both the stability of the surrounding community (Buffalo's Polish East Side) and the practical importance of public markets before the supermarket era. The building itself is part of Western New York's heritage, and several serious preservation and revitalization efforts have worked to ensure it remains a functioning market rather than becoming a museum or being redeveloped.

Absolutely not — the market is open to everyone and actively welcomes visitors of all backgrounds. The Easter season especially draws visitors from across WNY who grew up with no connection to Polish culture but who have heard about the market from neighbors or family friends and want to experience it. Many of the vendors are delighted to explain what they're selling, where the traditions come from, and how to use what you're buying. It's also genuinely kid-friendly during the Easter season — the hand-painted eggs and lamb cakes are appealing to children regardless of their family's heritage.
