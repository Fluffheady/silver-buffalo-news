The Broadway Market at Easter: What It Was, What It Still Is, and Why You Should Go This Year

By Vinnie DiPaolo
Nostalgia & Home Editor
April 19, 2026  ·  5 min read  ·  📍 Buffalo, NY Last updated by the Editorial Board: April 19, 2026

📋 Quick Facts

  • The Broadway Market in Buffalo's Broadway-Fillmore neighborhood opened in 1888, making it one of the oldest continuously operating public markets in the United States.
  • The Easter season — roughly the two weeks before Easter Sunday — is the market's biggest event of the year, drawing tens of thousands of visitors from across WNY and the diaspora.
  • The market's Easter specialty vendors include kielbasa makers, horseradish grinders, hand-painted pysanky egg artists, babka bakers, and sellers of lamb cake molds and forms.
  • Easter 2026 fell on April 5th. If you missed it this year, Easter 2027 falls on March 28th — mark your calendar now.
  • The Broadway Market is located at 999 Broadway in Buffalo's East Side. It is open year-round, not just at Easter, though the holiday season is its cultural peak.

My grandmother on my father's side was not Polish. She was Sicilian, same as most of the DiPaolos who came through Buffalo. But every year, the week before Easter, she would take me on the bus down to the Broadway Market, and she'd spend two hours making her way through every booth like she was on a diplomatic mission to a foreign country she genuinely admired.

She'd buy kielbasa she didn't strictly need. She'd taste the horseradish and make a face. She'd admire the hand-painted eggs without buying them, then circle back and buy two. "The Polish ladies," she'd say on the bus home, with a respect in her voice she didn't extend to many things. She meant it as the highest possible compliment.

I thought about her a lot when I was down at the Broadway Market this past Easter season. April 5th was Easter Sunday this year, and the market in the days before it was exactly what it has always been — and also, quietly, a little bit less so. That tension is what I want to write about.

What the Broadway Market Is

The building at 999 Broadway opened in 1888 — that's not a typo, not a rounding. 1888. The same year Buffalo was still processing the aftermath of the Great Blizzard of 1888, which hit just weeks after the market opened. Grover Cleveland had just finished his first term as president. The market is older than cars, older than the radio, older than the Skyway and Main Place Mall and most of the institutions that Buffalonians think of as permanent.

It sits in the Broadway-Fillmore neighborhood on the East Side, which was the heart of Buffalo's Polish community for most of the 20th century. At its peak, the neighborhood around the market was a dense, working-class Polish Catholic world — the kind of place where the church, the market, and the social club were the three pillars of daily life, and where knowing which parish you attended told people everything they needed to know about you.

The Broadway Market was that neighborhood's pantry. Not just at Easter — year-round. Meat markets, produce, baked goods, dry goods, specialty items you couldn't find anywhere else in the city. But Easter was always its Super Bowl, to borrow a term.

What Easter at the Market Actually Looks Like

If you've never been to the Broadway Market during Holy Week, I'll try to describe it, though description only gets you so far.

The kielbasa vendors are the first thing that hits you — the smell of smoked sausage and garlic is aggressive and wonderful, and it starts before you're even inside the building. The good vendors grind their own and smoke it on-site, and the difference between that and what you buy in the grocery store is not subtle. This is not Wegmans kielbasa. Wegmans kielbasa is fine. This is something else.

The horseradish grinders are their own category. Fresh-ground horseradish — the kind that clears your sinuses from six feet away — is a staple of the Polish Easter table, and the market vendors grinding it fresh are operating at a level of potency that no jarred product comes close to matching. My eyes were watering the first time I got near the horseradish booth at the age of maybe twelve, and they still water every year. That's how you know it's right.

Then there are the eggs. The pysanky — hand-painted Ukrainian Easter eggs — are something that has to be seen to be understood. Some of the designs are geometric, traditional, hundreds of years old. Some are more recent, more personal. The women who make them are working with beeswax and dye and techniques that their grandmothers' grandmothers used. You can buy one for a few dollars from a table that looks like it's been set up the same way since 1955, because in most essential respects, it has been.

And then there's the lamb cake. Every Polish Easter table needs a lamb cake — a sweet cake baked in a ceramic or cast-iron mold in the shape of a lamb, frosted white, usually decorated with a ribbon and sometimes a small flag. The molds themselves are sold at the market, alongside the cakes. They are also, I should mention, extremely collectible — old cast-iron lamb cake molds from the 1940s and 1950s sell at antique markets for $40 to $100. I found two of them at an estate sale in Cheektowaga a few years back and had no idea what I was looking at until I asked a woman at the market.

The Part That's Hard to Say

Easter 2026 came and went — April 5th this year, and if you missed it, I'm sorry, but I also want you to hear this: don't miss it next year. Easter 2027 falls on March 28th. Put it on the calendar right now.

Here's the part I struggle to write without sounding morbid, but it needs to be said. The women who run many of these booths — the kielbasa vendors, the pysanky painters, the ladies selling kapusta and ćwikła and babka — are getting older. Not old in a casual sense. Old in the sense that the generation of Polish and Ukrainian grandmothers who learned these trades from their own grandmothers is the last generation that learned them that way, in that unbroken chain. Some of them are in their eighties. Some have daughters who will continue. Some don't.

This isn't doom and gloom for its own sake. The Broadway Market is still there. It's still open. The Easter season still draws big crowds. The city has invested in the building, and there are genuine efforts to bring in new vendors and new energy to the market year-round. Some of those efforts are working. The market in 2026 is not a museum, not a dying institution — it's a living one.

But the specific thing that makes Easter at the Broadway Market irreplaceable — the actual Babcia behind the table, the actual recipe that came over from Kraków or Lviv, the actual hands that have been painting eggs for sixty years — that specific thing is finite in a way the building is not. And finite things deserve your time while they're still here.

My grandmother was right to make those trips, year after year, to pay her respects to the Polish ladies. I didn't fully understand it then. I understand it now.

Go next year. Bring somebody younger than you. Explain what you're tasting and where it comes from. That's the whole point.

Frequently Asked Questions

The Broadway Market at 999 Broadway in Buffalo is open year-round, though hours vary by vendor and season. The Easter season — typically the two weeks before Easter Sunday — is when the market is at its fullest and most festive. The Thursday and Friday of Holy Week are peak days; if you want to see the market at its busiest and most traditional, those are your best bets. Easter 2027 falls on March 28th. The market typically ramps up Easter activity in the second week of March.

The essentials for the traditional Polish Easter basket (święconka) that gets blessed at church: kielbasa, smoked ham, a boiled egg, a small piece of bread, salt, and horseradish. The market provides all of these, often at quality you simply cannot find elsewhere. Beyond the basket items: babka (a traditional Easter bread), pysanky (hand-painted eggs) as gifts or decorations, and if you're ambitious, a lamb cake mold and the ingredients to make your own. Budget a few hours, wear comfortable shoes, and arrive hungry.

The Broadway Market opened in 1888, making it one of the oldest continually operating public markets in the United States — older than most American institutions people think of as historic. Its longevity reflects both the stability of the surrounding community (Buffalo's Polish East Side) and the practical importance of public markets before the supermarket era. The building itself is part of Western New York's heritage, and several serious preservation and revitalization efforts have worked to ensure it remains a functioning market rather than becoming a museum or being redeveloped.

Absolutely not — the market is open to everyone and actively welcomes visitors of all backgrounds. The Easter season especially draws visitors from across WNY who grew up with no connection to Polish culture but who have heard about the market from neighbors or family friends and want to experience it. Many of the vendors are delighted to explain what they're selling, where the traditions come from, and how to use what you're buying. It's also genuinely kid-friendly during the Easter season — the hand-painted eggs and lamb cakes are appealing to children regardless of their family's heritage.
